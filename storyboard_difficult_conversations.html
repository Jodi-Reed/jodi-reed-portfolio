Learning Objectives

  • Recognize the cost of avoiding difficult conversations in the workplace
  • Apply the SBI (Situation–Behavior–Impact) model to structure a difficult conversation
  • Demonstrate preparation strategies that reduce emotional reactivity before the conversation
  • Use active listening and neutral language to keep a difficult conversation on track
  • Identify the appropriate follow-up actions after a difficult conversation concludes

02
The Cost of Avoidance
Content
LO 1 — Recognize the cost
Visual / Layout
Layout Three-column icon layout on white background. Each column has a simple line icon above a stat or insight. Navy headline. Teal accent on column dividers. Subtle animation: columns fade in left-to-right on load.
On-Screen Text
What Happens When We Don't Say Anything?
  • Trust erodes. Teams notice when managers don't address problems. Silence sends a message — just not the one you intend.
  • Issues escalate. Small problems become bigger ones when left unaddressed. A missed deadline becomes a pattern. A comment becomes a habit.
  • The relationship suffers anyway. Avoidance doesn't protect the relationship — it just delays the damage and adds resentment.

The goal of a difficult conversation is not to win — it's to stay in the relationship while addressing what matters.
Narration Script
It's tempting to convince ourselves that staying quiet is the kind thing to do. But avoidance has a cost — to the team, to the person we're not addressing, and to us. Unspoken issues create distance. They build into frustration. And eventually, they come out anyway — usually at the worst possible moment, and in a way we didn't intend. The good news? Having the conversation — done well — almost always makes things better.
Interaction Notes
Linear. Learner clicks "Next" to advance. Consider adding a brief click-to-reveal interaction: each of the three points is hidden behind a clickable tile that flips to reveal the insight.
Facilitator / Developer Notes
Source: HBR "How to Handle Difficult Conversations at Work" (Rebecca Knight, 2015). Keep icons simple and professional — avoid anything that reads as conflict or confrontation. Tone of this screen should feel empathetic, not judgmental.
03
Introducing the SBI Model
Content — Framework
LO 2 — Apply SBI
Visual / Layout
Layout Horizontal three-step flow diagram. Each step is a rounded rectangle: S (blue), B (purple), I (green). Arrows connect each step. Below each step: label + definition + example phrase. Animated: steps appear one at a time as learner scrolls or clicks.
On-Screen Text
A Framework That Works: SBI
S — Situation B — Behavior I — Impact
  • Situation: When and where did this happen? Be specific. "In yesterday's team meeting..."
  • Behavior: What did you observe? Stick to facts, not interpretations. "...you interrupted Marcus three times before he finished his point."
  • Impact: What was the effect? On the team, the work, or you. "I noticed he stopped contributing for the rest of the meeting, and I'm concerned it's affecting team engagement."

SBI keeps the conversation grounded in facts — not feelings, assumptions, or personality judgments.
Narration Script
The SBI model gives you a clear, respectful structure for any difficult conversation. Situation grounds the feedback in a specific moment — not a pattern accusation. Behavior describes what you observed, not what you assumed about the person's intent. And Impact explains why it matters — to the team, to the work, or to you. Together, these three elements make the conversation feel fair and factual rather than personal or attacking.
Interaction Notes
Click-to-reveal: learner clicks each letter (S, B, I) to expand its definition and example phrase. All three must be clicked before "Next" becomes active. This ensures engagement with the framework before moving on.
Facilitator / Developer Notes
SBI model sourced from Center for Creative Leadership. Use a workplace scenario that is realistic but neutral — avoid protected characteristics. The interrupting example is intentionally low-stakes. Consider providing a downloadable SBI job aid at the end of this screen.
04
Before the Conversation: Prepare to Show Up Well
Content — Preparation
LO 3 — Preparation strategies
Visual / Layout
Layout Two-column layout. Left: a brief checklist graphic with checkboxes (interactive). Right: a short explanatory paragraph for whichever item the learner hovers or selects. Warm neutral background. Subtle paper texture.
On-Screen Text
Preparation Is the Conversation You Have With Yourself First Before you sit down with the other person, take time to:
  • Clarify your intent. Are you trying to punish, or to improve? Your intent will shape your tone — and they'll feel it.
  • Write out your SBI. Draft the Situation, Behavior, and Impact before you speak. Edit out any language that sounds blaming or absolute ("you always," "you never").
  • Anticipate their reaction. How might they respond? What would you say if they get defensive? Having a plan reduces reactivity in the moment.
  • Choose the right time and place. Private, calm, unhurried. Never right before a big deadline or at the end of a stressful day.
Narration Script
The most important conversation you'll have happens before you ever walk into the room. When you prepare — when you get clear on your intent, draft your SBI, and think through how the other person might respond — you show up regulated instead of reactive. And that changes everything. Research from Harvard Business Review shows that managers who prepare for difficult conversations report significantly better outcomes, both in resolution and in relationship quality afterward.
Interaction Notes
Interactive checklist: learner clicks each item to reveal a brief explanation (tooltip or accordion). All four items must be opened before "Next" activates. Optional: include a "Download Prep Checklist" button linking to a PDF job aid.
Facilitator / Developer Notes
Source: HBR "How to Handle Difficult Conversations at Work" (Rebecca Knight, 2015) and "How to Have Difficult Conversations Without Burning Bridges" (Evelyn Nam, 2023). Emphasize that preparation is about clarity, not scripting. Managers should not read from notes in the actual conversation.
05
Meet Jordan: A Manager Who Needs to Act
Scenario Setup
LO 2 + LO 4 — Apply SBI / Listen actively
Visual / Layout
Layout Character-based scenario layout. Left: illustrated avatar of "Jordan" (manager) and "Sam" (direct report) in a virtual meeting frame. Right: scenario description text on light warm background. Narrative tone — like a case study card.
On-Screen Text
The Situation Jordan is a manager on a cross-functional team at a mid-sized healthcare organization. Sam, one of Jordan's direct reports, has been missing deadlines and arriving late to team meetings for the past three weeks. Other team members have noticed, and Jordan has been asked about it twice this week.

Jordan has been putting off the conversation, worried about upsetting Sam, who is generally a strong performer and seems to be having a hard month personally.

Today, Jordan decides it's time.

Using the SBI model, Jordan has prepared the following:
SBI
  • Situation: "Over the past three weeks, across four of our team meetings..."
  • Behavior: "...you've arrived late or logged off early, and two project deliverables have come in past the agreed deadline."
  • Impact: "The team has had to adjust timelines twice, and I've noticed some frustration starting to build among the group."
Narration Script
Let's follow Jordan through this conversation. Notice how Jordan uses the SBI framework to open — specific, behavioral, and impact-focused. There's no character judgment, no "you always," no mention of personal struggles. Just what Jordan observed and why it matters. Now comes the harder part: Sam responds. And the next choice Jordan makes will determine how the rest of the conversation goes.
Interaction Notes
Linear. Learner reads Jordan's SBI script, then clicks "Next" to see Sam's response and the branching decision point. Consider audio option: recorded dialogue between Jordan and Sam playing as learner reads along.
Facilitator / Developer Notes
Avatars should be gender-neutral or clearly diverse. Healthcare setting is intentional for BCH audience relevance. Avoid naming or implying any personal struggles (health, family) for Sam — the scenario should remain behaviorally focused per the SBI model guidance.
06
Sam Gets Defensive — What Does Jordan Do?
Branching Decision
LO 4 — Active listening & neutral language
Visual / Layout
Layout Dialogue bubble from Sam displayed prominently at top. Below: two response buttons, side by side — Option A and Option B. Each button shows Jordan's response text. No icons or color coding until after the learner chooses (to avoid telegraphing the answer).
On-Screen Text + Branching
Sam responds: "I don't think that's fair. I've been working harder than anyone on this team. If anything, people should be looking at what everyone else isn't doing." How should Jordan respond? Choose the best option:
⬡ Decision Point — Select One
Option A
"I understand you feel that way, but I need you to focus on your own performance right now. The rest of the team is not what we're here to discuss."
→ Result: Sam shuts down. Conversation ends without resolution. Relationship strained.
Option B ✓
"I hear that you feel you've been putting in a lot of effort — that matters, and I want to understand your perspective. Can you help me understand what's been getting in the way of the deadlines?"
→ Result: Sam feels heard. Conversation shifts to problem-solving. Relationship preserved.
Interaction Notes
Branching: learner selects Option A or B. Option A shows a brief consequence screen (Sam disengages, conversation fails) with a "Try Again" button that returns to this screen. Option B advances to Screen 7. Both paths rejoin at Screen 7 after feedback.
Facilitator / Developer Notes
Source: HBR "How to Have Difficult Conversations Without Burning Bridges" (Evelyn Nam, 2023) — key insight: acknowledging emotion does not mean agreeing with the behavior. Option B demonstrates the "acknowledge and redirect" technique. Option A is not malicious — it's the instinctive response most managers reach for. The feedback on Option A should be empathetic, not critical of the learner.
07
After the Conversation: What You Do Next Matters
Reflection + Application
LO 5 — Follow-up actions
Visual / Layout
Layout Timeline graphic: three horizontal stages (Immediately After / Within 24 Hours / Ongoing). Each stage has a distinct icon and 1–2 action items. Clean, airy layout on white. Soft green accent color throughout to signal completion and forward movement.
On-Screen Text
The Conversation Isn't Over When It Ends
  • Immediately after: Give yourself — and them — space to decompress. Don't follow up the same hour. The other person needs time to process.
  • Within 24 hours: Send a brief, neutral written summary of what was discussed and agreed upon. This is not a reprimand — it's a shared reference point. Keep it factual and forward-focused.
  • Ongoing: Follow through on anything you committed to. If you said you'd check in next week, check in next week. Consistency after a hard conversation rebuilds trust faster than the conversation itself.

Reflection prompt:
Think of a difficult conversation you've had or observed in the past. What happened after it ended? What would you do differently using what you've learned today?
Narration Script
What happens after a difficult conversation is as important as the conversation itself. Giving the other person room to process — and then following up with a calm, written summary — signals that you're serious about moving forward together, not scoring points. And when you follow through on what you said you'd do, you rebuild trust in a way that's hard to manufacture any other way. The conversation plants the seed. What you do after is how it grows.
Interaction Notes
Optional typed reflection field: learner can type a brief response to the reflection prompt. Response is private and not submitted — it's a personal processing tool. Include a "Skip" option. After reflecting, learner clicks "Next" to reach the final summary screen.
Facilitator / Developer Notes
Source: HBR "You Just Had a Difficult Conversation at Work. Here's What to Do Next" (Dolores Bernardo, 2017). The written follow-up recommendation is especially important in regulated environments (like healthcare) where documentation matters. Frame the summary email as a shared reference, never as a formal warning unless HR is involved.

Summary & Knowledge Check
Summary + Assessment
All LOs
Visual / Layout
Layout Two sections on same screen: (1) Key Takeaways card in navy, with teal bullet accents. (2) Below: single multiple-choice knowledge check question with four response options. Submit button activates after selection. Certificate/completion badge appears on correct answer.
On-Screen Text
What to Carry Forward
  • Avoiding a difficult conversation doesn't protect the relationship — it delays damage and adds distance.
  • The SBI model (Situation–Behavior–Impact) gives you a factual, fair structure that focuses on behavior, not character.
  • Preparation — especially writing out your SBI and anticipating reactions — reduces reactivity in the moment.
  • When someone gets defensive, acknowledge their emotion before redirecting to the issue.
  • A brief written follow-up after the conversation creates a shared reference and rebuilds trust.

Knowledge Check Jordan uses the SBI model to open a difficult conversation. Sam responds defensively. What is the most effective next step for Jordan?

A. Restate the SBI feedback more firmly so Sam understands the seriousness
B. End the conversation and schedule a follow-up with HR present
C. Acknowledge Sam's feelings and ask an open question to understand their perspective
D. Remind Sam that other team members have noticed the issue
Narration Script
Difficult conversations are one of the most human things a manager does. They require courage, preparation, and a genuine desire to stay in the relationship even when things get uncomfortable. The SBI model won't make every conversation easy — but it will make every conversation fairer. And that's where trust gets built. Take a moment to review the key takeaways, then test yourself with a quick knowledge check before you go.
Interaction Notes
Knowledge check: correct answer is C. Feedback on each option: A — explains why restating more firmly often escalates defensiveness. B — premature escalation. C — correct, explains why. D — bringing in others is a deflection technique, not effective. Two attempts allowed before correct answer is revealed. Module completes and marks as finished in LMS on screen close.

Include a downloadable job aid on this screen: a one-page SBI Conversation Prep Sheet (fillable PDF). Completion triggers LMS completion status.
